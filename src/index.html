On the other end of the line, Cecilia lay crumpled, shot in the head at age 35 by her drunken ex-boyfriend, who immediately turned the gun on himself.

The last call of her life had been the eighth she made to 911 in a span of nine hours.

From the evening of Oct. 9, 2014, to the early morning of Oct. 10, Lam called repeatedly from the South of Market apartment she shared with four roommates, asking for police to protect her from 29-year-old Cedric Young Jr., with whom she was engaged in a volatile breakup.

After many of her calls, officers sought to help, but they failed to take actions that might have kept her alive.

A Chronicle examination of the case — including interviews with Cecilia’s roommates and family, police reports, 911 recordings and court depositions given by police officers and other witnesses — reveals a series of questionable actions by authorities that contributed to a breakdown in a system designed to prevent such domestic violence killings, a system that has been a focus in San Francisco for years.

The events the night of Cecilia’s murder underscore the complexity of intimate violence and the challenges officers face in intervening. In many domestic violence cases, one of the biggest hurdles to overcome is the mindset of the victim, who is caught up in the shame of being abused and is often unwilling to reach out to law enforcement until it is too late.

But Cecilia called for help. Help came. The question that remains, three years later, is why it wasn’t enough to save her. It’s a question her family has pursued, so far unsuccessfully, in court, hoping to force the Police Department to re-evaluate how its officers respond to domestic violence.

The Police Department has declined to comment on the case, but department officials say the city has come a long way in creating effective policies for responding to domestic violence calls, and in training officers to recognize when a situation may be more complicated and dangerous than it seems.

John Coté, a spokesman for the city attorney’s office, said that while Cecilia’s death was a tragedy, “Ultimately, the responsibility for Cecilia Lam’s death lies with the person who committed this senseless crime.”

But like the stubborn, spirited woman they miss every day, the family isn’t giving up.

 

“I would like, and my family would like, for this not to happen to anyone else,” said Eddie Lam, the victim’s younger brother. “We know bad things happen to good people every day. But I don’t want her to be just another number. I want people to understand that this didn’t have to happen this way.”

 

The night Cecilia died began like many others had in the second-floor apartment on the 500 block of Natoma Street in the South of Market neighborhood. At about 7 p.m., Michael Ramirez, one of Cecilia’s four roommates, returned home to find her and Young in the kitchen, arguing.

Their four-year relationship, which had that long been turbulent, had become consumed by Young’s alcohol abuse.

“They were constantly fighting,” roommate Nikki McKay recalled. “You get home and they’re fighting. She’d come into my room and we’d talk about it and it was always just about nonsense. She wanted to hang out with her friends, and he didn’t want that. She wanted to go to her parents’ house, and he would say, ‘Why do you have to go to your parents’ house all the time?’”

The couple had met in 2010, in a chance encounter at a Walgreen’s. People were drawn to the vivacious young woman, and Young was no exception. He approached her and struck up a conversation. He was tall, handsome and, at the time, gentle.

Cecilia called up her childhood best friend, Mario Lieras, to announce: “I met a boy!”

Lieras figured Young was going to be just another fling, but Cecilia had told her mother of a desire to settle down. She wanted a family, too.

Her parents, Joseph and Shut-Fan, had emigrated from Hong Kong more than three decades ago, when Cecilia was 5 and Eddie was 1. Their daughter took on a burden often bestowed upon the eldest children in first-generation American families. She was not only a cultural bridge for her parents, but a mother figure to her younger siblings, making sure they experienced the American norms that were so unfamiliar to their parents.

Cecilia was 10 when brother Sunny was born. She called him her “baby” and her “boy.” He still jokingly refers to her as his “moms.”

Cecilia had ambitions, but she delayed them in order to put her family first. In 2013, she returned to school after a long absence, pursuing a degree in Asian American studies at San Francisco State University. She hoped to do civil rights work, advocating for women and Asian Americans.

“In her spare time,” chuckled Lieras, “she also wanted to cure cancer. She wanted to be a little power Asian woman and do all the things.”

Young, who went by the name Jay, was a San Francisco native who had moved to Chicago as a child after his parents divorced. He grew up with potential, said aunt Victoria Wallace. He was a sweet boy and a nerd at heart, and though he tried to fit in and pretended to be “street,” he couldn’t hide his intelligence, she said. An honor student through high school, he earned a scholarship to Alabama A&M University in Huntsville.

But he dropped out of college following a fight and began running afoul of the law. His father, hoping to straighten him out, brought him back to live in California when he was in his early 20s.

At some point, he turned to drugs and alcohol. He was arrested three times, twice on suspicion of assault and battery, but was never charged because the victims refused to cooperate. He was in an addiction treatment program when he and Cecilia met. But when the two began dating, things clicked, Wallace said.

“They were always holding hands, very affectionate,” Wallace said. “If they were standing, she was right under his arm. You know how you can look at a couple and say, ‘Wow, they are in love!’ That’s how it was.”

 

But Young’s violent tendencies surfaced not long after he moved into Cenilia’s Natoma Street apartment . He got in fights. He punched holes in the walls and broke a door.

On Valentine’s Day in 2012, Young got drunk and accused his girlfriend of cheating. Two neighbors in the unit below came upstairs when they heard Young tearing up the kitchen. He shoved Lam twice, pushed one of the neighbors and choked the other, according to police reports.

One of the neighbors took out a restraining order against Young. But when Cecilia reported the incident the next day, she told officers she didn’t believe Young had intended to hurt anyone, that she just wanted “documentation.” None of the victims were willing to cooperate, and although Young was arrested for felony assault and domestic violence, he wasn’t charged.

A few weeks before the night of the shooting, McKay, whose room shared a wall with Cecilia’s, recalled hearing the couple arguing — then heard what sounded like choking. Cecilia didn’t report any abuse, but soon after, Young was gone from the apartment, and Cecilia told her relieved roommates they had broken up for good.

In early October, though, Lam informed her roommates Young was moving back. She later said he had been homeless following the break-up, and she felt bad for him. Her roommates objected, but on Oct. 9, 2014, there he was.

 

A year before Cecilia’s death, an outside (CLARIFY) district attorney’s office reviewed San Francisco’s domestic violence prosecutions. The inquiry was spurred by allegations (BY WHOM?) that District Attorney George Gascón was not charging enough cases.

The review found that prosecutors couldn’t bring charges against some purported abusers because of the way police patrol officers responded to domestic violence calls.

“As a generalization, the patrol level investigations tend to be extremely brief and the burden is transferred to the (police) Inspector to conduct a more detailed investigation,” the Riverside report stated.

The city had been working for (HOW MANY?) years to improve its handling of domestic violence cases after the killing of Claire Tempongko. In the 18 months before her death, the 28-year-old had called police, filed reports and sought protective orders, only to be stabbed by an ex-boyfriend in front of her two children on Oct. XX, 2000.

A commission formed after her death found that almost every part of the criminal justice system had failed her, and the city sought to ensure that such a tragedy would not happen again. In some ways, the effort was successful, as city and state agencies collaborated with victim advocates and nonprofit groups. Cecilia’s murder was the first domestic violence homicide in the city in four years.

Today, patrol officers receive several days of domestic violence response training at the academy, with a refresher course every two years. The department’s Special Victims Unit began a new type of training that allows officers to shadow inspectors in order to better undertand what evidence is needed to support criminal charges.

“I’m a believer in if people understand more why they’re doing things, they get better at doing it,” said Police Capt. Una Bailey, who heads the Special Victims Unit.

This past June, the department launched a pilot program out of Bayview Station in which patrol officers responding to domestic violence calls ask victims a specific series of questions to determine the seriousness of each case and put the victims in immediate contact with advocates.

The program may appear commonsense. But for some victims of domestic violence, the psychological effects of the abuse prevents them from realizing the danger they are in until it’s too late.

Many are like Cecilia: trapped in a seemingly endless cycle of shame, co-dependency, conflicting emotions and violence .

 

Cecilia, Young and her roommates lived on the second floor of a three-flat building, in an alley that was being transformed with stylish condos . But their 110-year-old building was gray and fading, with a broken iron entry gate that led to a short stairwell and the front doors of the three units.

The middle door opened to a flight of stairs leading up to Cecilia’s apartment. On the evening of Oct. 9, she stood at the top of those stairs, yelling down at Young. She’d kicked him out again, and was screaming at him to go away.

Outside, Young was banging on the door to their unit and ringing the bell. At 8:37 p.m., Cecilia made her first call to 911.

“My boyfriend and I, we’re pretty much fighting right now and I’m asking him to leave my home,” she told the dispatcher. “And he will not leave ... it’s starting to escalate.”

Young had been drinking all day, she said, then quickly added, “He’s actually leaving now.”

“I’m getting more scared,” Cecilia said in the third call. “I don’t know if he’s going to break in.”

As they spoke, Tov later said, Lam came out and defended Young, saying, “He’s a good guy. He doesn’t cause any trouble. He just gets this way sometimes.”

But Ramirez felt that despite her attempts to downplay the situation, Cecilia was deeply frightened. He tried to intervene, presenting the pasta sauce and forcing Cecilia to acknowledge that she believed the sauce had been poisoned. In response to his intervention, Ramirez recalled that the officer “sort of did this half shrug. He laughed kind of, kind of did this thing like, ‘What am I going to do with that?’ sort of gesture.”

Tov’s report said Cecilia denied Young had tried to poison her. He said she didn’t appear ill, so he didn’t mention the allegation to his partner. Neither officer sought to interview Ramirez at length.

Victims often refuse to speak out against abusers once authorities intervene, domestic violence experts say. The Police Department’s general order on domestic violence states that officers should not be influenced by a victim’s “initial reluctance regarding an officer-initiated arrest.”

Tov would later testify that he asked Lam if Young had abused her in the past. She mentioned the Valentine’s Day incident in which he’d been arrested, but said the couple had moved on from it.

The officers did not run a background check on Young, which would have shown them not only that arrest but previous assault arrests, which experts said should have raised red flags about his potential for violence.

Neither officer considered the visit a domestic violence call, citing the lack of injury and that Cecilia did not appear frightened. By doing so, they did not initiate several steps laid out in department policy. They did not offer Cecilia a victim referral card listing resources should she need them, nor did they inform her of her right to make a citizen’s arrest.

After about 15 minutes in the alley, the officers watched Young walk away from the building, then left themselves. Young would not be gone for long.

 

The Cecilia that family members and friends knew and loved was unafraid to speak up and unwilling to be disrespected or bullied. That’s why the relationship she’d continued with Young baffled so many of them.

She hid the bad parts from her parents, reluctant to make them worry. But after the 2012 incident in which Young was arrested, she called her brother Eddie. And like she’d done for them so many times before, her brothers came together to support her.

Eddie told her, “You’ve always made the right decisions. I trust that you will make the right decision now.”

“She always taught me, you never lay your hands on a woman,” he said. “I was just hoping that Ceci would see for herself. All the things she preached about what I should never do to a woman was something she experienced. But for some reason, her heart just didn’t want to let that go.”

Complicating matters, Young appeared to be using drugs and alcohol to mask untreated mental illness, said his aunt. About a month before the murder-suicide, he told her, “Aunty, when I am in the house by myself, I hear voices.”

Another time, Young told his uncle he saw a figure in a window, making faces at him. A couple months before his death, he called his father to say he had had put a pistol in his mouth and was going to shoot himself.

His family never thought he was serious in these instances, but looking back, Wallace said she realized he had been quietly asking for help. Maybe he could have gotten that help, Wallace said, had the police heeded Cecilia’s calls.

“This could have been stopped,” she said. “He could have been alive today. They both could have been alive today.”

For the first time since they had all lived together on Natoma Street, the five roommates sat down together.

Ceci had been master tenant since about 2006, filling the rooms with an ever-changing roster of friends, acquaintances and Craigslist strangers. This latest set of roommates got along, but were rarely home at the same time. That night, they rallied around Cecilia, making each other drinks in the kitchen.

As her relationship with Young had devolved, so had the apartment. Cecilia was in charge of cleaning the shared spaces, but rarely did. The walls were yellow from her chain-smoking. The officers who responded to her 911 calls that night— Tov, Lobsinger, Haskell, Lee and Ha — are still with the city force.

Capt. Bailey of the department’s special victims unit said the city has made significant progress in its handling of domestic violence calls since the stabbing of Tempongko in 2000. City agencies, nonprofits and victim advocates collaborate now in developing policy, finding funding and providing resources for victims.

Included in those reforms is a revised department general order on domestic violence. A comprehensive policy, it addresses the many complexities of domestic violence cases, said Nick Casper, an attorney representing Cecilia’s family.

It requires responding officers to look beyond a victim’s reluctance to cooperate and instructs them to consider the victim’s history of complaints. It defines domestic violence as a crime beyond a visible injury.

The policy went into effect two days before Cecilia died.

The officers who responded would have been aware of it, Casper said. But having such a policy means nothing when officers refuse to view a call as domestic violence, as those involved in the Lam case admitted to in their court depositions.

“I think dispatch did everything they could,” Casper said. “They took down what Cecilia was telling them, they coded it appropriately. Cecilia in her calls even used the ‘magic language,’ calling it an ‘escalating domestic violence situation.’ But when the officers were presented with this situation that did not have the most overt signs of domestic violence, their response seemed to be, ‘How do I get out of here and take the next call?’ rather than, ‘How can I help this citizen who felt she was in enough danger to call 911?’”

In 2015, a police crime lab analyzed the the pasta sauce that Cecilia believed Young had poisoned. The sauce, which was finally taken into evidence after the murder-suicide, tested positive for rat poison.

The Lam family sued the city in federal court in 2015, seeking damages and hoping to force the Police Department to re-evaluate how its officers respond to domestic violence. This past June, Magistrate Judge Kandis Westmore dismissed the case. Public entities are protected from liability regarding injury or death caused by purported failure to protect, and the allegation that the officers created more danger by allowing Young into the apartment and possibly accessing the firearm lacked evidence, Westmore wrote in her decision.

The family decided not to appeal the ruling. But the dismissal in civil court does not absolve the city, Casper said.

“Whether something is legally actionable and whether it is morally wrong are unfortunately two different questions,” Casper said.

Investigators from other agencies said officers who respond to reports of domestic violence must be thorough. They need to talk to witnesses, look into the couple’s history and past interactions with authorities, and try to connect the victim with services — actions that for the most part did not happen before Cecilia’s death.

Upton, of the Domestic Violence Consortium, said law enforcement agencies nationwide, including in San Francisco, have come a long way in their view of domestic violence. But it’s not enough, she said.

It remains one of the few crimes in which the reaction is often to first question the seemingly self-destructive choices of the victim, Upton said. She said police should be asking better questions: What did he do to her to make it so hard for her to get help? What are the circumstances making her feel that she can’t leave?

Cecilia Lam’s death demonstrates just how complicated and frustrating domestic violence really is, Upton said. Cecilia Lam called for help. She called for help eight times. And still she was murdered.
